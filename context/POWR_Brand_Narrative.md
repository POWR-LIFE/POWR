## 01 — StoryBrand (Donald Miller)

> POWR is never the hero. The user is the hero. POWR is the guide.

### The Seven-Part Framework

1. **Character (The Hero)** — The person who already shows up. They gym, run, ride — consistently, silently, without recognition.
2. **External Problem** — Effort goes untracked and unrewarded. Especially inside the gym — the last uncharted territory in fitness.
3. **Internal Problem** — "I work harder than most people realise. Why doesn't that count for anything?"
4. **Philosophical Problem** — In a world of rewards apps, the most dedicated movers get the least back.
5. **The Guide (POWR)** — Empathy: we know effort is invisible. Authority: we built the only platform that traces the gym floor.
6. **The Plan** — Move → get verified → earn points → redeem real rewards from brands that respect the grind.
7. **Call to Action** — Start earning from your next workout.

| Success — With POWR | Failure — Without POWR | One-Liner |
|---|---|---|
| Every session builds toward something real. The streak matters. The effort pays off. | Keep grinding with nothing to show for it. Another year of invisible effort. | The gym is the last uncharted territory in fitness rewards. POWR changes that. |

**Use for:** homepage hero, pitch decks, gym partner conversations, investor narrative.

## 03 — Hero's Journey (Joseph Campbell)

> Maps the user's emotional arc from first awareness to brand advocacy.

### The User Arc

| Stage | Description |
|---|---|
| 1 — Ordinary World | Consistent mover. Shows up. Gets results. Effort is invisible to everyone else. |
| 2 — Call to Adventure | "What if your consistency was worth something beyond fitness gains?" |
| 3 — Refusal | "Another app? I've tried loyalty schemes before. They never stick." |
| 4 — Meeting the Mentor | POWR. Simple, dark, premium. Feels different from day one. |
| 5 — Crossing the Threshold | First gym visit tracked. First points earned. The streak begins. |
| 6 — Tests & Allies | Building the streak. Discovering partner rewards. Sharing with gym friends. |
| 7 — The Ordeal ⚠️ | The streak at risk. Loss aversion kicks in. **The single most important retention moment.** |
| 8 — The Reward | First redemption. Something tangible earned from invisible effort. |
| 9 — The Road Back | Identity shift: "I'm a POWR user." Movement is not just health — it is an asset. The streak is their record. |
| 10 — Return with the Elixir | "You're not on POWR? You're leaving points on the table." This is the organic growth moment. |

> **Dev/Product Note — Stage 7 (The Ordeal):** The streak at risk is the most important retention mechanic. Loss aversion at this moment is why the streak multiplier is not just a gamification feature — it is a core narrative beat. Every design decision around streak warnings, forgiveness windows, and recovery should be built around this emotional stage.

**Use for:** onboarding screen copy, push notifications, lifecycle email arc, product milestone design.

## 04 — Brand Archetype (Carl Jung)

### Primary: The Ruler
Control. Premium. Order. Creates systems that reward those who operate at the highest level. Aspirational but not exclusionary — standards matter.
**Reference brands:** Equinox, Amex Black, Rolex.

### Secondary: The Explorer
Pushing limits. Discovering new territory. POWR is doing what no one else has done — mapping the gym floor.
**Reference brands:** Strava, Patagonia, The North Face.

### Voice Guidelines

| Tone of Voice | POWR Says | POWR Never Says | Visual Language |
|---|---|---|---|
| Direct. Premium. Never shouty. Confident without arrogance. | "Every move counts." / "The streak is yours." / "Earned, not given." | "Crush it!" / "Hustle harder!" / "Beast mode." — Motivational-poster energy is off-brand. | Dark. Cinematic. Gold accent. Outfit font. Always dark backgrounds. Equinox-meets-Strava. |

> **Copy test:** If it sounds like a Nike ad from 2015, it is wrong for POWR.

## 05 — Category Design (Al Ries + Play Bigger)

> POWR doesn't just need to win in an existing market — it needs to define a new category that it owns. The brand that defines the category almost always wins it.

### The Competitive Map

| Strava | Sweatcoin | POWR |
|---|---|---|
| Owns outdoor running and cycling. Strong community, social layer. Does not touch the gym floor. | Owns steps. Mass market, advertising model. No gym presence. | **Owns the gym floor — and all movement rewards. The gap nobody else has claimed.** |

> **The category POWR owns: Gym Workout Rewards. First mover. Nobody else is here.**

The Strava model is the proof of concept. Strava started by owning one high-intent vertical — outdoor running — and scaled into everything else. POWR starts by owning the gym floor — the single highest-intent, highest-frequency fitness behaviour — and scales from there.

| Short-Term (Now) | Long-Term Vision |
|---|---|
| Win the gym floor. EC1 / Shoreditch launch. Founding Partners. May 2026. | POWR is the OS for rewarding all movement. The gym floor is the beachhead. |

**Use for:** investor deck, gym partner pitch, PR narrative, competitive positioning conversations.

## Always. Never.

### Always
- Lead with the user, not the product
- Use confident, direct language
- Acknowledge effort before asking for anything
- Treat the gym floor as the primary differentiator
- Speak to consistency, not motivation

### Never
- Use hustle-culture language — crush it, grind, beast mode
- Lead with features before problems
- Use generic wellness-app language
- Shout. Over-explain. Claim to be the hero.

### For Users
The hero is always the person who already shows up. POWR recognises what was already happening — it does not ask them to change behaviour.

### For Gym Partners
The hero is the gym operator who cares about their members. Never lead with churn stats at premium operators — lead with member recognition.

## A Note on Execution

This document is the strategy. The execution lives in every line of copy on the site, every push notification in the app, every cold email to a gym operator, every social post, and every product decision about what to build next.

The positioning only works if the product and the communication are consistent. A premium dark UI with hustle-culture copy is a contradiction. A category-defining claim without gym floor traceability to back it up is a liability. Strategy and execution have to move together.
